# Treehouse Research Foundation

This document grounds our product decisions in behavioral science and child psychology research. Every major feature should be traceable back to evidence.

## 1. Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan)

**Source:** [selfdeterminationtheory.org](https://selfdeterminationtheory.org/theory/)

### Core Insight
Human motivation thrives when three basic psychological needs are met:

| Need | Definition | How to Support |
|------|------------|----------------|
| **Autonomy** | Sense of initiative and ownership in one's actions | Provide choice, minimize external control |
| **Competence** | Feeling of mastery, sense that one can succeed and grow | Well-structured challenges, positive feedback |
| **Relatedness** | Sense of belonging and connection | Convey respect and caring |

### Key Findings
- Autonomy is "undermined by experiences of being externally controlled, **whether by rewards or punishments**"
- Children internalize behaviors when they feel secure and cared for
- Students taught with controlling approaches "not only lose initiative but learn less effectively"
- Supports for autonomy and competence "incite more intrinsic motivation"

### Treehouse Application
- **Growth XP** = feedback on competence, not currency to spend (preserves autonomy)
- **Choice in tasks** = kid picks which chores, how to approach them
- **Family kudos** = builds relatedness through recognition
- **Personal progress** = competence feedback without comparison

## 2. The Danger of External Rewards (Alfie Kohn)

**Source:** [Punished by Rewards](https://www.alfiekohn.org/punished-rewards/)

### Core Insight
Rewards and punishments are two sides of the same coin—both are ways of **doing things TO** people rather than **working WITH** them.

### Key Findings
- "The more we use artificial inducements to motivate people, **the more they lose interest** in what we're bribing them to do"
- "Intrinsic motivation is often **killed by extrinsic motivation**"
- Rewards are **most damaging when the task is already intrinsically motivating**
- Even praise can become "a verbal bribe that gets kids hooked on our approval"

### Kohn's Alternative: The 3 Cs
1. **Collaboration** - Working together, not competing
2. **Content** - Understanding the reasoning behind actions
3. **Choice** - Room for individual autonomy

### Treehouse Application
- **No spendable points** - XP is feedback, not currency
- **No prize shop** - Can't "buy" rewards with points
- **Careful praise design** - Praise process, not person
- **Explain the "why"** - Help kids understand purpose of tasks

### Important Nuance
Some research suggests rewards contingent on creativity can actually increase creative performance. The key is **how** rewards are structured, not whether they exist at all.

## 3. Growth Mindset (Carol Dweck)

**Source:** [Stanford Research](https://bingschool.stanford.edu/news/carol-dweck-praising-intelligence-costs-childrens-self-esteem-and-motivation)

### Core Insight
How you praise children shapes their relationship with challenge and failure.

### The Landmark Study
Children given an IQ test were praised differently:
- **Intelligence praise:** "You must be smart at this!"
- **Effort praise:** "You must have worked really hard!"

**Results after a subsequent failure:**
- Intelligence-praised kids did **20% worse** on final test
- Effort-praised kids did **30% better** on final test

### Key Findings
- Intelligence praise puts kids in a **fixed mindset**
- Kids praised for intelligence chose easy tasks (to keep looking smart)
- Kids praised for effort chose challenging tasks (to learn more)
- Growth-minded individuals "bounce back by increasing their motivational effort"

### Important Clarification from Dweck
> "A growth mindset isn't just about effort... Students need to try new strategies and seek input from others when they're stuck."

Praising effort for unproductive struggle isn't helpful. Praise the **process**: effort + strategies + focus + perseverance + improvement.

### Treehouse Application
- **Praise process, not talent** - "You kept trying" not "You're so good"
- **Celebrate strategies** - "You tried a different approach"
- **Normalize struggle** - Difficulty is part of growth
- **Bounce-back celebration** - Returning after failure = key skill

## 4. Gamification: When It Helps vs. Harms

**Source:** [PMC Meta-Analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10591086/)

### Overall Findings
Meta-analyses show gamification has **medium to large positive effects** on learning outcomes (g = 0.5-0.8), but effects vary significantly.

### When Gamification HELPS
- Younger children (elementary) benefit more than older
- Points, progress bars, and feedback have medium-large effects
- **Enhances perceived autonomy** (g = 0.638) and **relatedness** (g = 1.776)
- Fosters enthusiasm and goal-setting
- Provides feedback on performance

### When Gamification HARMS or Fails
- **Minimal impact on competence** (g = 0.277) - critical need unmet
- Can cause **anxiety or jealousy** (especially leaderboards)
- Less effective when it "doesn't bring additional utility"
- Effects on motivation are less stable than cognitive effects
- College students benefit less than younger students

### Treehouse Application
- **Progress feedback: YES** - Visual growth, stats, levels
- **Competition: CAREFUL** - Personal progress over leaderboards
- **Points for utility: NO** - Don't trade points for things
- **Autonomy preservation: CRITICAL** - Choice in how to engage

## 5. Resilience and Bouncing Back

**Sources:** [Positive Psychology](https://positivepsychology.com/what-is-resilience/), [Adaptable Discipline](https://adaptable-discipline.com/guides/the%20comeback%20model)

### Core Insight
Resilience isn't just "not breaking"—it's the ability to **recover and reconfigure** after difficulty.

### Three Components of Resilience
1. **Recovery** - Returning to baseline after stress
2. **Resistance** - Limited disturbance during stress
3. **Reconfiguration** - Finding new stability after change

### Neuroscience of Bouncing Back
- Brain responds to failure within 100ms (cingulate cortex)
- **When you return to a habit after lapse, dopamine increases**
- Brain rewards the act of re-entry, not streak completion
- This conditions the brain to **value the comeback itself**

### The Emotional Decay Curve
The longer you stay away from a broken habit, the **harder it gets to return**. Avoidance creates compounding emotional barriers.

**Implication:** Make re-entry as frictionless as possible.

### Self-Compassion and Recovery
- Self-compassion is strongly linked to resilience (Kristin Neff research)
- Shame **lengthens comeback time**
- Compassionate self-talk reduces emotional resistance
- People who practice self-compassion "try again after failure" more readily

### Treehouse Application
- **"Bounce back" > "never break streak"** - Celebrate returns
- **Built-in rest days** - Prevent all-or-nothing thinking
- **No shame for lapses** - Gentle re-entry prompts
- **Multiple metrics** - Best streak + current streak + comeback count
- **Quick recovery path** - Low friction to restart

## 6. Journaling for Children & Adolescents

**Sources:** [Child Mind Institute](https://childmind.org/blog/the-power-of-journaling/), [Crown Adolescent Health](https://crownadolescenthealth.com/the-power-of-journaling-a-therapeutic-tool-for-teen-mental-health/)

### Research-Backed Benefits
- Journaling can be **as effective as CBT** in reducing depressive symptoms in high-risk adolescents
- Meta-analysis: 5% average symptom reduction, **9% for anxiety**, 6% for PTSD
- Enhances neural processing in areas related to cognitive control and memory
- "Cognitive defusion" - helps see thoughts as separate from self

### Benefits for Kids
- Safe space to process emotions
- Reduces intensity of difficult emotions
- Tracks worries to identify patterns
- Builds self-awareness
- Improves mood and reduces stress

### Age-Appropriate Considerations
| Age | Approach |
|-----|----------|
| Younger (6-10) | Ready-made prompts, fillable journals, simple questions |
| Tweens (10-13) | Mix of prompts and free writing, mood tracking |
| Teens (13+) | More freedom, voice/video options, deeper reflection |

### Effective Prompt Types
- Gratitude: "What are you grateful for?"
- Pride: "What made you proud today?"
- Struggle: "Something that was hard this week"
- Goals: "Tomorrow I want to..."
- Emotions: "How do you feel right now?"

### Privacy is Critical
> "If a child finds it difficult to communicate about thoughts and feelings externally, a journal can be a trusted, unconditional tool that gives no judgment."

Children's journal privacy must be respected for the tool to work.

### Treehouse Application
- **Optional daily check-in** - 30-60 seconds, not required
- **Age-appropriate prompts** - Different for 10yo vs 16yo
- **Privacy by default** - AI summaries for parents, not raw content
- **Multiple input modes** - Text, voice, possibly images
- **Reflection = Wisdom XP** - Journaling builds stats

## 7. Extrinsic → Intrinsic Transition

**Sources:** [Ryan & Deci](https://selfdeterminationtheory.org/wp-content/uploads/2020/04/2020_RyanDeci_CEP_PrePrint.pdf), [ASU Research](https://asu-ir.tdl.org/bitstreams/018805bd-1475-4929-9916-1acd040fd028/download)

### The Core Question
Can external rewards **scaffold** the development of intrinsic motivation, then fade away?

### Key Findings
- **Yes, but carefully.** "Appropriately designed extrinsic motivation systems can scaffold the development of more autonomous forms of motivation."
- **Gradual fading is essential:** "Systematically reducing extrinsic reinforcement over time while building internal motivation supports the internalization process."
- Internalization enhanced when:
  - Evaluative aspects are de-emphasized
  - Students feel control over environment
  - Activities are challenging yet doable
  - Rationale for activities is provided

### The Overjustification Effect (Warning)
Offering extrinsic rewards for initially intrinsically motivating activities can **reduce internal motivation** once rewards are removed.

### Developmental Note
- Younger students respond more positively to extrinsic motivation
- Older students develop greater capacity for autonomous regulation
- **Implication:** Scaffolding may be more appropriate for younger kids in our range (10-12), fading for older (14-16)

### Treehouse Application
- **XP as feedback, not reward** - Shows growth without creating dependency
- **Explain the why** - "This builds your Focus stat because..."
- **Celebrate intrinsic moments** - "You did this without being asked!"
- **Graduation goal** - Explicitly aim for the app to become unnecessary
- **Age-adaptive** - More scaffolding for younger, more autonomy for older

## Summary: Research-Backed Design Principles

| Principle | Research Basis |
|-----------|---------------|
| Growth XP, not spendable points | Kohn (rewards undermine), SDT (autonomy) |
| Personal progress, not leaderboards | Gamification research (anxiety/jealousy) |
| Bounce-back celebration | Resilience research (dopamine on return) |
| Built-in rest days | Emotional Decay Curve (prevent shame) |
| Process praise | Dweck (20-30% performance difference) |
| Optional journaling | Child Mind Institute (as effective as CBT) |
| Privacy by default | Journaling research (trust required) |
| Explain the "why" | SDT (internalization), Kohn (content) |
| Gradual scaffolding → fading | SDT internalization research |
| Success = graduation | Intrinsic motivation as end goal |
